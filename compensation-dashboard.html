Pay Inversion & Compression

How to read these three
  • Level inversion (structural flag). An individual's base exceeds the median actual base of the level directly above, within the same country and function (GMT area). A cohort-level signal of structural pressure — not, on its own, a confirmed person-level inversion.
  • Person-level inversion (the textbook case). A report out-earns their own performance manager — the pay relationship actually flips. The clearest, most defensible inversion signal.
  • Compression (the milder cousin). Differentials narrow below what the structure intends without flipping — e.g. a manager paid less than 1.15× their reports' median.
All three compare pay within the same country only — cross-border pairs aren't comparable across differing markets and FX. Tests use median actual base as the comparator (placeholder salary bands power the separate Salary Ranges tab); adjacent pay ranges are designed to overlap, so the meaningful bar is the level-above median, not its minimum. A level-inversion flag can occur while the person still earns less than their own manager, which is why it and the person-level test are shown separately.

Pay structure & internal equity
  • Level-progression differentials. Median % step-up in base from each level to the one above, computed within each country then aggregated across them. Healthy structures keep consistent differentials; a thin or negative step signals compression or inversion at the cohort scale.
  • Pay mix (bonus leverage). Bonus as a share of base by level. Variable pay should lever up with seniority.
  • Compa-ratio spread. Each person's base ÷ the midpoint of their salary band (see the Salary Ranges tab). Centred on 1.0 when pay tracks the midpoint — the width of the spread shows how tightly each level is managed; below 0.8 or above 1.2 are outliers worth review.
Compa-ratio now uses the placeholder band midpoints from the Salary Ranges tab; differentials use median actual base within each country (countries with ≥ 3 people at both adjacent levels). Both are FX-invariant.
